\begin{abstract}
We study the problem of estimating the vertical distance travelled by an
elevator from a single smartphone, end-to-end. The system has two stages
that operate on the raw inertial stream and share the same
config-dispatcher architecture. \textbf{(1) Segmentation \& detection}
slices a continuous, possibly hours-long, sensor session into discrete
\texttt{up} / \texttt{down} ride intervals. Our production detector is
accelerometer-only and is built on a matched filter against the
trapezoid pulse-pair predicted by the jerk-limited S-curve kinematic
model; a barometer-based detector runs in parallel and is the
ground-truth source whenever the phone has a pressure sensor.
\textbf{(2) Prediction} takes each detected ride and returns a signed
$\Delta h$ in metres together with a calibrated 90\% confidence
interval. Three prediction algorithms share a common
\texttt{predict / calibrate} contract: a barometer baseline (ISA
inversion), a Zero-Velocity-Update (ZUPT) double integrator, and a
7-step S-curve velocity-domain fitter. Every accelerometer-based
predictor composes a per-segment theoretical noise budget $\sigma^2$
(white-noise ZUPT drift, mechanical jitter, distance-proportional scale
term, projection-quality penalty, and, for the S-curve, Cram\'er--Rao
information on the distance parameter) with a split-conformal
calibrator fitted on the clean training pool. The result is a
per-segment 90\% CI that adapts to ride duration, gravity stability,
and fit quality. Quality filtering at the prediction stage is
accelerometer-only (no barometer, no orientation fusion). The two
stages are exposed as a single user-facing tool, the
\textbf{Boutique Pipeline}, a Streamlit wizard that walks an operator
through ingestion, manual segment review, prediction, and the export
of a Hebrew per-ride PDF report. On the held-out test location
(\texttt{beitYitzchaki}, never seen at training time) the system
retains its nominal coverage guarantees while the median CI half-width
remains well below the $\pm 1.5$~m one-floor target on short rides and
grows gracefully on longer rides. Every number in the results section
is generated by \texttt{scripts/run\_prediction\_evaluation.py} and the
figure bundle under
\texttt{src/data/structuredData/test\_results/prediction/}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

A smartphone's vertical motion carries the signature of an elevator
ride in its raw inertial data --- but only if one is willing to commit
to (a) a kinematic model of how the ride is generated, (b) a
\emph{detector} that turns hours-long continuous sensor streams into
discrete ride intervals without missing too many or hallucinating new
ones, (c) an \emph{estimator} that maps each detected ride to a signed
height difference, (d) an error model that quantifies how noisy each
measurement can be, and (e) a calibration step that turns these models
into coverage-guaranteed confidence intervals on real field data. This
report documents the end-to-end pipeline we built for all five.

\paragraph{Two stages, one architecture.} The system is organised as
two pipeline stages that share the same shape: a Pydantic config
selects one of several algorithms, and a single dispatcher class
(\texttt{Segmenter} / \texttt{Predictor}) exposes the public method of
the stage (\texttt{.detect} / \texttt{.predict}).

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Segmentation \& detection
    (\S\ref{sec:segmentation-detection}).} Take a continuous sensor
    session and slice it into discrete ride intervals tagged
    \texttt{up} / \texttt{down} / \texttt{outside}. Our production
    detector is \emph{accelerometer-only} and is built on a
    matched filter that scans the signal for trapezoid pulse-pairs
    predicted by the jerk-limited S-curve kinematic model
    (\S\ref{sec:elevator-kinematics}); a paired-lobe filter then
    decides which pairs of candidate lobes constitute a genuine ride.
    A pressure-filter detector runs in parallel as a ground-truth
    source on phones that carry a barometer.
  \item \textbf{Prediction (\S\ref{sec:height-prediction}).} Take each
    detected ride and return a signed $\Delta h$ in metres with a
    per-segment 90\% confidence interval. Three algorithms share a
    common \texttt{predict / calibrate} contract: a barometer baseline
    (ISA inversion), a Zero-Velocity-Update (ZUPT) double integrator,
    and a 7-step S-curve velocity-domain fitter.
\end{enumerate}

Reusable, stage-agnostic helpers live in \texttt{src/utils/}. Ground
truth comes from the barometer when the phone has one (PRS sensor
$\to$ ISA altitude $\to$ labelled ride intervals); experiments without
a barometer use hand-labelled intervals edited through the GT editor
(\S\ref{sec:gt-editor}).

\paragraph{The user-facing tool.} The two stages are exposed together
as the \textbf{Boutique Pipeline}, a Streamlit-based wizard that walks
an operator from raw signal to a Hebrew per-ride PDF report in five
steps (data ingestion, interactive segmentation, prediction, export).
The pipeline is described in detail in
\S\ref{sec:boutique-pipeline-guide}; the surfaces a non-research user
will actually touch are an \emph{interactive segmentation review}
where every detector proposal can be accepted, edited, removed, or
augmented with manually-added rides, and a \emph{report exporter}
that writes a per-segment audit trail.

\paragraph{Roadmap.}
\S\ref{sec:data-collection} describes the data collection: seven
elevators across six buildings, four phones per ride, and the Python
loader that converts raw \texttt{sensorLog\_*.txt} files into tidy
per-sensor CSVs with ground-truth intervals.
\S\ref{sec:segmentation-detection} introduces the kinematics of an
elevator ride --- the four-parameter jerk-limited S-curve model ---
and the two segmentation pipelines: the barometer-based ground-truth
detector and the accelerometer-only matched-filter detector that ships
in production. It also covers the calibration of the eight detection
thresholds, the pair-filter hyperparameters, and the per-experiment
evaluation. \S\ref{sec:height-prediction} is the prediction
contribution of this work: the two accelerometer-only algorithms,
their quality filters, their theoretical per-segment $\sigma$ budgets,
and their shared split-conformal calibration.
\S\ref{sec:evaluation-protocol} describes the cross-building
train/test split, the clean-vs-dirty distinction in the ground truth,
and the metrics we target. \S\ref{sec:prediction-results} reports the
train-set and blind-test results;
\S\ref{sec:prediction-discussion} discusses what they imply for the
next iteration of the pipeline.
\S\ref{sec:boutique-pipeline-guide} is a user-facing guide to the
Boutique Pipeline UI: workflow, interpretation of the heatmaps and
correlation panels, when to manually edit segments, and how to read
the exported report.

\subsection{Experimental Design}
\label{sec:experimental-design}

campaign was concentrated in Ra'anana (four buildings, five elevators),

\paragraph{Phones.}
In every ride we simultaneously recorded data from \textbf{four phones}.
Using four devices per ride serves two purposes. First, it gives us
heterogeneous sensor hardware on a single, perfectly synchronised physical
event, which lets us study cross-device variance (different MEMS
accelerometers, different sampling jitter, different barometer quality)
without confounding it with different rides. Second, it guarantees that at
least one of the four devices carries a \textbf{barometric pressure sensor}
that we can use as a ground-truth source, even when one of the target
phones under evaluation does not have one. Typical device mix across the
campaign:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{SamsungSM-S911B} --- Galaxy S23 (has barometer).
  \item \texttt{GooglePixel10} / \texttt{GooglePixel} --- Pixel family (has barometer).
  \item \texttt{Xiaomi22101320I} --- Redmi Note 12 family (no barometer).
  \item \texttt{SamsungSM-A235F} --- Galaxy A23 (no barometer).
  \item \texttt{GalaxyZFlip6} --- foldable (has barometer).
\end{itemize}

The experimenters (\texttt{eyalyakir}, \texttt{UriyaCohenEliya},
\texttt{RoyTurgeman}) carried the devices together in a single hand or bag
so that all four phones experience nearly identical motion, vibration, and
acoustic environment during a ride.

\paragraph{Folder and file naming.}
Every recording is stored under\\
\texttt{\small src/data/rawData/\allowbreak{}<experimenter>\_\allowbreak{}<location>\_\allowbreak{}<phone>\_\allowbreak{}<date>\_\allowbreak{}exp<N>/}\\
to make the origin of every sample unambiguous from the path alone. This
convention is documented in \texttt{src/data/README.md} and is enforced only
by convention --- the loader (\S\ref{sec:loader}) does not parse it, but
every analysis notebook groups by it.

\subsection{Logging: The Barometer}
\label{sec:logging-app}

On every phone, a sensor-logging Android application subscribes to every
inertial and environmental sensor the device exposes and dumps every
sample, verbatim, to a tab-separated text file
\texttt{sensorLog\_<ISO-timestamp>.txt}. One row per sample, no header,
format:
\begin{center}
  \texttt{<timestamp\_ms>\textbackslash t<SENSOR>\textbackslash t<v$_1$>\textbackslash t<v$_2$>...}
\end{center}
The sensors we rely on are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:sensor-schema}.

The most important channel for ground truth is the \textbf{barometer
(\texttt{PRS})}. The pressure sensor is sampled in parallel with the
other sensors and is the sole signal the elevator detector
(\S\ref{sec:elevator-detection}) acts on. Because not every device in the
fleet has a barometer, we attach a secondary device (typically a Pixel or
S23) that \emph{does} have one to every ride whose primary phone is
pressure-less; its recording lives in a \texttt{forBarometer/} sub-folder
under the primary experiment's directory. This secondary-device trick is
central to the time-alignment problem discussed in \S\ref{sec:time-sync}.

Once the recording is over, the raw dump is committed to
\texttt{rawData/<exp\_name>/}, together with a hand-filled
\texttt{metadata.txt} (experimenter, phone, location, description, date,
time). The loader pipeline (\S\ref{sec:loader}) then materialises a CSV
per sensor under \texttt{structuredData/data/<exp\_name>/} and derives
\texttt{gt.csv} --- the ground truth used by every downstream algorithm.

\subsection{Elevator Detection}
\label{sec:elevator-detection}

Ground truth for this project is a labelling of the timeline into
\texttt{up}, \texttt{down}, and \texttt{outside} intervals: ``outside''
means the phone is not on a moving elevator (waiting, walking, idle). The
labelling is derived algorithmically from the barometer signal and then
optionally corrected by hand (\S\ref{sec:gt-editor}).

\subsubsection{Barometer-Based Algorithm}
\label{sec:barometer-algorithm}

The detector lives in
\texttt{\small src/algorithms/\allowbreak{}segmentation\_algorithms/\allowbreak{}barometer\_only/\allowbreak{}height\_segmentation.py}
and runs in six stages. Inputs: a time series $(t_i, p_i)$ of timestamps
and pressure readings.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Altitude.} Convert pressure to altitude via the
    international barometric formula,
    \[
      h_i = \frac{T_0}{L}\,\Big[1 - \big(p_i/p_0\big)^{RL/(gM)}\Big],
    \]
    implemented in \texttt{src/physics/pressure\_to\_altitude.py}. This
    removes the weather-dependent DC offset only approximately, but the
    segmenter depends on \emph{differences} in $h$, so the residual bias
    cancels.

  \item \textbf{Low-pass smoothing.} Apply a centered rolling-mean filter
    of width \texttt{height\_lowpass\_sec}~$\cdot f_s$ samples to suppress
    high-frequency pressure jitter (door slams, HVAC bursts).

  \item \textbf{Vertical velocity.} Estimate $v_z$ by forward differencing
    the smoothed altitude, $v_{z,i} = (h^{\text{lp}}_{i+1} - h^{\text{lp}}_i)/\Delta t_i$,
    then box-smooth again over \texttt{smooth\_window\_sec}.

  \item \textbf{Thresholding.} Mark samples where
    $|v_z| > \texttt{velocity\_threshold}$ (default $\approx 0.15$~m/s)
    as ``moving''. Extract contiguous runs of \texttt{moving} samples by
    edge-finding on the binary mask.

  \item \textbf{Merging.} Walk the runs in order and merge consecutive
    runs of the \emph{same sign} (both \texttt{up} or both \texttt{down})
    if the gap between them is shorter than \texttt{merge\_gap\_sec}. This
    collapses spurious velocity dips caused by elevator deceleration mid-ride.

  \item \textbf{Pruning and padding.} Drop any surviving run whose
    duration is $\le$ \texttt{min\_duration\_sec} or whose total altitude
    change is $\le$ \texttt{min\_height\_diff\_m}. Expand every surviving
    run by \texttt{pad\_sec} on both ends so the label comfortably covers
    the full ride, including the small door-open tail.
\end{enumerate}

The output is a \texttt{DataFrame} of rows \texttt{(start\_ci, end\_ci,
duration, type, probability\_ci)} where \texttt{type} $\in$
\{\texttt{up}, \texttt{down}\}. The pipeline then pads the gaps between
these rows with \texttt{outside} intervals so that the GT timeline is
contiguous --- this is what downstream evaluation code expects.

\paragraph{Why this is good enough as ground truth.} A well-calibrated
consumer barometer on a phone resolves pressure changes equivalent to
roughly 10~cm of altitude at typical sampling rates, which
is an order of magnitude below the smallest interesting inter-floor height
($\sim$3~m). The algorithm is deterministic, so repeated runs
on the same recording produce identical labels, which in turn makes
regression testing tractable.

\subsection{The Time-Synchronisation Problem}
\label{sec:time-sync}

The reason Section~\ref{sec:elevator-detection} alone does not close the
loop is a low-level Android timing issue that we spent considerable time
getting right.

\paragraph{The root cause.} Android's
\texttt{SensorEvent.timestamp} is the time at which the sensor event was
recorded, but it is expressed relative to the \emph{device boot time}
(\texttt{SystemClock.elapsedRealtimeNanos}), not to wall-clock. The
logger writes this timestamp directly into the log file. Consequently, the
\texttt{timestamp\_ms} column in \texttt{sensorLog\_*.txt} is a
\emph{device-local uptime} value, not a UTC time. Two phones that were
booted at different moments will assign different timestamps to the
\emph{same} physical event, with the offset equal to the difference of
their boot times --- in our dataset, frequently tens of thousands of
seconds, sometimes days.

\paragraph{Why this bites us in practice.} Recall
(\S\ref{sec:logging-app}) that phones without a barometer rely on a
secondary device's \texttt{PRS} stream for ground truth. To use the
secondary device's altitude as ground truth for the primary device, we
must answer: \emph{at primary timestamp $t_p$, what was the altitude?}
That requires mapping primary time to secondary time, which requires
knowing the boot-time offset between the two devices. Neither device
records wall-clock, so the offset must be recovered from the data itself.

\paragraph{First approximation: start-time alignment.} The simplest
workable estimate is to assume both phones started recording at the same
wall-clock moment, so the first sample of each log corresponds to the
same real time. Under that assumption, the offset is
\[
  \Delta = t^{(\text{prim})}_0 - t^{(\text{sec})}_0,
\]
and we shift every secondary timestamp by $\Delta$ before using it. This
is implemented in
\texttt{src/data/loader/\allowbreak{}alignment.py\allowbreak{}::\_compute\_start\_offset}
and is sufficient when the experimenter hits ``start'' on both phones
in quick succession.

\paragraph{Why start-time alignment is not enough.} In practice the user
latency between the two taps is one to several seconds and is variable
between rides. That multi-second bias shifts the ground truth by enough
to invalidate per-sample evaluation (an elevator moves at $\sim 1.5$~m/s;
a 2~s timing error translates to $\sim 3$~m of altitude
error, more than an entire floor). We therefore refine the offset using
the \textbf{accelerometer signal}, which is recorded by both phones and
must be approximately identical for two devices in the same hand.

\paragraph{Refinement by cross-sensor correlation.} The routine used in
the lab is: (i) compute the magnitude $|a|(t)$ on both phones, (ii)
resample both to a common grid after applying the first-pass start-time
offset, (iii) run a normalised cross-correlation of the two $|a|$ traces
over a bounded lag window ($\pm 10$~s was enough in every
experiment we observed), and (iv) pick the lag that maximises the
correlation as the residual offset to fold back into $\Delta$. If ACC is
missing on either device, we fall back to gyroscope magnitude or
heuristic event matching on step-count spikes. The diagnostic overlay
(Figure~\ref{fig:forBarometer-alignment}) is produced automatically for
every experiment with a \texttt{forBarometer/} companion and is the first
thing to inspect when an alignment looks suspicious.

\paragraph{Practical hand-offs.} The offset is computed once per
experiment, logged, and baked into the secondary's \texttt{PRS} frame
before it is swapped into the primary's sensor dictionary
(\texttt{\_merge\_secondary\_prs} in \texttt{alignment.py}). Everything
downstream --- the segmenter, the GT derivation, evaluation metrics ---
therefore sees \emph{a single phone} with a valid barometer, which
removes a whole class of timing bugs from the rest of the stack.

\subsection{The Loader Pipeline}
\label{sec:loader}

\texttt{src/data/loader/} exposes a narrow public API
(\texttt{getExperimentData}, \texttt{saveExperimentData},
\texttt{list\_experiments}) that hides all of the above. On first access
it parses the raw \texttt{sensorLog\_*.txt}, merges any
\texttt{forBarometer/} secondary, runs the detector
(\S\ref{sec:barometer-algorithm}), pads the timeline with
\texttt{outside} intervals, and writes per-sensor CSVs plus
\texttt{gt.csv} to \texttt{structuredData/data/<exp\_name>/}. On every
subsequent call it reads those CSVs directly. To force a rebuild after
code changes, pass \texttt{use\_cache=False}. The CSV schemas are
documented in \texttt{src/data/README.md} and frozen in
\texttt{loader/constants.py}.

\subsection{The Ground-Truth Editor}
\label{sec:gt-editor}

No matter how careful the detector in \S\ref{sec:barometer-algorithm} is,
it will occasionally miss the start of a ride, split a single ride into
two, or classify an HVAC-induced pressure ripple as movement. Rather than
chase every edge case in the algorithm, we decided early on that the
ground truth must be \emph{auditable and editable} by a human. Every
\texttt{gt.csv} in the dataset has therefore passed through the GT editor
at least once.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{gt_editor.png}
\caption{The GT editor. Left pane: stacked matplotlib panels (altitude,
vertical velocity, $|a|$, $|\omega|$, $|m|$) with ground-truth intervals
drawn as coloured spans (green $=$ up, red $=$ down, grey $=$ outside).
Right pane: a \texttt{Treeview} of all intervals with the edit form

\label{fig:gt-editor-main}

\paragraph{Launching.} The editor is a Tkinter application:
\begin{verbatim}
venv/bin/python -m src.data.gt_editor [exp_folder_name]
\end{verbatim}
Called without arguments, it lists all experiments found under
\texttt{rawData/} in a dropdown and lets the user pick one.

\paragraph{Visualisation.} The left pane renders all five standard motion
signals on a shared time axis. Ground-truth intervals are overlaid on
every panel simultaneously so a user can sanity-check a label against
multiple modalities at once (e.g.\ a true elevator ride must be
accompanied by a clear altitude step \emph{and} a flat-ish accelerometer
magnitude).

\paragraph{Editing.} The interaction model is designed to keep the mouse
near the signal rather than in a form. Concretely:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Click} anywhere inside a coloured span to select that
    interval in the right-hand tree.
  \item \textbf{Drag an edge} (within a few pixels of the start or end
    of a span) to resize it live; the cursor morphs into a horizontal
    arrow while inside the hit-tolerance zone.
  \item Type precise millisecond bounds in the \textbf{edit form}, change
    the \texttt{type} (\texttt{up} / \texttt{down} / \texttt{outside})
    or the free-text \texttt{note}, and hit \textbf{Apply}.
  \item \textbf{$+$~Add} inserts a new 10-second \texttt{outside}
    interval right after the current selection.
  \item \textbf{$\times$~Delete} removes the current interval.
  \item \textbf{Auto-fix} closes gaps between adjacent intervals with
    \texttt{outside} filler so the timeline stays contiguous; it refuses
    to merge if two intervals actually overlap, to avoid silently
    destroying user edits.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Navigation.} Keyboard shortcuts mirror the matplotlib toolbar:
\texttt{+}~/~\texttt{=} zoom in, \texttt{-} zoom out, \texttt{0} fit to
full range; \texttt{Shift+$\leftarrow$} / \texttt{Shift+$\rightarrow$}
pan by 25\% of the visible width; mouse-wheel over the plot zooms around
the cursor. All of this is indispensable on long recordings where a
single ride is only a few percent of the timeline.

\paragraph{Saving.} \texttt{Ctrl+S} (or the \textbf{Save} button) calls
\texttt{saveExperimentData}, which rewrites every per-sensor CSV,

\section{Segmentation \& Detection}
\label{sec:segmentation-detection}

This section covers how a continuous accelerometer (and, when
available, barometer) stream is sliced into elevator-ride segments. We
begin with the underlying physics of an elevator ride --- a four-
parameter motion model that every detector in this work eventually
reduces to --- and then describe the concrete detectors built on top.

\subsection{Background: Elevator Kinematics}
\label{sec:elevator-kinematics}

Before describing any detector we lay down the physics of an elevator
ride, because the same four parameters reappear in every algorithm we
use later. Modern passenger elevators are commanded by a
\emph{jerk-limited motion profile} so that passengers experience a
smooth, bounded change of acceleration (see, e.g., the ride-comfort
specifications summarised in ISO~18738 and the manufacturer
literature aggregated in \texttt{papers/elevator\_research\_links.txt}).
The drive obeys, in order, the constraints
\begin{equation}
  \big|\dddot{h}(t)\big| \le j_{\max}, \qquad
  \big|\ddot{h}(t)\big| \le a_{\max}, \qquad
  \big|\dot{h}(t)\big|  \le v_{\max},
  \label{eq:elevator-bounds}
\end{equation}
where $h(t)$ is the cabin altitude, $\dot{h}=v$ the vertical velocity,
$\ddot{h}=a$ the acceleration measured by the phone (after gravity
removal), and $\dddot{h}=j$ the jerk. Typical commercial values are
$j_{\max}\!\approx\!1.0\,\text{--}\,1.6$~m/s$^3$,
$a_{\max}\!\approx\!0.8\,\text{--}\,1.5$~m/s$^2$, and
$v_{\max}\!\approx\!1.0\,\text{--}\,2.5$~m/s for low- and mid-rise
buildings (CIBSE Guide~D, 2020).

\paragraph{The seven-segment S-curve.} Saturating each constraint in
turn yields the canonical S-curve velocity profile (Figure
\ref{fig:elevator-kinematics}). The ride decomposes into up to seven
phases:
\[
\underbrace{j=+j_{\max}}_{\text{1: jerk up}}\;\;
\underbrace{j=0}_{\text{2: cruise }a_{\max}}\;\;
\underbrace{j=-j_{\max}}_{\text{3: jerk down}}\;\;
\underbrace{a=0}_{\text{4: cruise }v_{\max}}\;\;
\underbrace{j=-j_{\max}}_{\text{5: jerk down}}\;\;
\underbrace{j=0}_{\text{6: cruise }-a_{\max}}\;\;
\underbrace{j=+j_{\max}}_{\text{7: jerk up}}.
\]
Phase~4 collapses to zero length on short hops where the cabin never
reaches $v_{\max}$, in which case the profile is ``triangular'' in
acceleration; longer rides are ``trapezoidal''. The constituent
durations follow from \eqref{eq:elevator-bounds}:
\begin{equation}
  T_j = \frac{a_{\max}}{j_{\max}}, \qquad
  T_a = \max\!\left(0,\; \frac{v_{\max}}{a_{\max}} - T_j\right), \qquad
  T_v = \max\!\left(0,\; \frac{H}{v_{\max}} - T_j - T_a\right),
  \label{eq:elevator-durations}
\end{equation}
where $H$ is the floor-to-floor height. The total ride length is
$T_{\text{ride}} = 4 T_j + 2 T_a + T_v$.

\paragraph{What $T_j$, $T_a$, $T_v$ mean.} Each of the three durations
in \eqref{eq:elevator-durations} labels a distinct \emph{kinematic
regime} of the ride:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{$T_j$ -- jerk-limited ramp duration.} The time the
    drive spends at a \emph{saturated} jerk $|\dddot h|=j_{\max}$. This
    is the length of phases~1, 3, 5, and 7, during which the
    acceleration $a(t)$ is a straight line with slope $\pm j_{\max}$
    (linear ramp). Because the acceleration must grow from $0$ to
    $a_{\max}$ at slope $j_{\max}$, the duration is fixed by the
    machine: $T_j = a_{\max}/j_{\max}$. For typical commercial values
    ($a_{\max}{\approx}1.0$~m/s$^2$, $j_{\max}{\approx}1.3$~m/s$^3$)
    this is $T_j{\approx}0.77$~s.
  \item \textbf{$T_a$ -- constant-acceleration plateau duration.} The
    time the cabin spends at a \emph{saturated} acceleration
    $|a|=a_{\max}$. This is the length of phases~2 and~6, the flat
    tops (or bottoms) of the acceleration trace. $T_a$ is zero for
    short hops that never reach $v_{\max}$ (``triangular'' rides) and
    grows linearly with the peak velocity budget $v_{\max}/a_{\max}$
    once $v_{\max}$ is hit.
  \item \textbf{$T_v$ -- constant-velocity cruise duration.} The time
    the cabin spends at a \emph{saturated} velocity $|\dot h|=v_{\max}$
    with $a=0$. This is the length of phase~4, the flat top of the
    velocity profile between the two acceleration lobes. $T_v$
    vanishes on any ride where $H$ is too small for the cabin to
    finish accelerating before it must begin decelerating; when it is
    non-zero it scales linearly with the floor-to-floor height,
    $T_v\approx H/v_{\max} - T_j - T_a$.
\end{itemize}

simulated S-curve for a representative trapezoidal ride.

\paragraph{What the phone measures.} The phone's gravity-projected
linear acceleration $a(t)$ traces \emph{exactly} the bracketed
acceleration in \eqref{eq:elevator-bounds}, modulo sensor noise and
projection error. Three model facts are worth highlighting because the
detector exploits them:

\begin{itemize}
  \item During the four jerk phases ($T_j$ each), $a(t)$ is linear in
    time with slope $\pm j_{\max}$. In the velocity domain this
    integrates to a quadratic --- the parabolic ``ramp shoulders'' the
    user can see in Figures \ref{fig:elevator-kinematics} and
    \ref{fig:elevator-allrides}.
  \item The signed area under the first acceleration lobe equals the
    peak velocity reached:
    \begin{equation}
      v_{\text{peak}} \;=\; \int_{0}^{2T_j+T_a}\! a(t)\,dt
      \;=\; a_{\max}\,(T_j + T_a)
      \;=\; \min\!\left(v_{\max},\; \sqrt{a_{\max} H}\,\right)
      \quad\text{for short rides.}
      \label{eq:elevator-vpeak}
    \end{equation}
    The second (deceleration) lobe has equal magnitude and opposite
    sign, so $\int_{0}^{T_{\text{ride}}}\! a(t)\,dt = 0$. This is the
    fundamental reason ZUPT works: the cabin starts and ends at rest,
    so any non-zero terminal velocity in the integrated signal is
    drift, not motion.
  \item The temporal gap between the centroids of the two acceleration
    lobes is the cruise time $T_v + 2T_a + 2T_j \approx H/v_{\max}$
    when $v_{\max}$ is reached. \textbf{Hence the inter-pulse spacing
    is a direct, scale-free measurement of the floor-to-floor height
    $H$.}
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{The four-parameter ride model.} Combining
\eqref{eq:elevator-bounds}--\eqref{eq:elevator-vpeak}, every ride is
parameterised by the tuple
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\theta_{\text{ride}} \;=\; \big(j_{\max},\; a_{\max},\; v_{\max},\; H\big)}\;,
  \label{eq:elevator-params}
\end{equation}
with $j_{\max}, a_{\max}, v_{\max}$ being properties of the elevator
machine (constant within a building) and $H$ being the only ride-
specific quantity. Once $\theta_{\text{ride}}$ is fixed, the ideal
acceleration template is fully determined:
\begin{equation}
  a_\theta(t) =
  \begin{cases}
    +j_{\max}\, t, & 0 \le t < T_j, \\
    +a_{\max}, & T_j \le t < T_j + T_a, \\
    +a_{\max} - j_{\max}(t - T_j - T_a), & T_j + T_a \le t < 2T_j + T_a, \\
    0, & 2T_j + T_a \le t < 2T_j + T_a + T_v, \\
    \text{(mirror of phases 1--3, with opposite sign)}, & \text{otherwise.}
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:elevator-template}
\end{equation}
Velocity $v_\theta(t) = \int_0^t a_\theta(\tau)\,d\tau$ and
displacement $h_\theta(t) = \int_0^t v_\theta(\tau)\,d\tau$ are then
piecewise cubic and quartic respectively. Equation
\eqref{eq:elevator-template} is the synthetic template used by the
DTW-based detector (Algorithm~2 in \texttt{papers/algorithm2\_dtw.md})
and by the matched-filter pulse detector in
\texttt{template\_match/scripts/acc\_pulse\_detect.py}.

\subsection{Whole-Signal Trapezoid Detector}
\label{sec:trapezoid-detector}

Our production accelerometer-only detector works directly on the
idealised two-lobe model from Section~\ref{sec:elevator-kinematics}:
a ride is a signed acceleration pulse at take-off followed by an
opposite-sign acceleration pulse at landing. The algorithm is split
into two stages that mirror the two halves of the problem. A
\emph{detection} stage finds candidate lobes in the raw signal; a
\emph{pair-filter} stage decides which pairs of lobes constitute
genuine rides. Eight scalar thresholds --- four in each stage ---
plus four template-grid bounds are bundled in a single configuration
object, so a sweep can move them all in lockstep.

\subsubsection{Tunables and How We Calibrated Them}
\label{sec:trapezoid-tunables}

Before diving into the stages themselves it helps to have every
tunable on one page. The detector exposes two kinds of knobs:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Template-grid bounds} that define the set of
    trapezoidal templates $\tau_{W,f}$ swept over the signal
    (Section~\ref{sec:trapezoid-detect}, Equation
    \eqref{eq:trap-template}). These shape \emph{what} the matched
    filter can match.
  \item \textbf{Acceptance thresholds}, four in the detection stage
    and four in the pair filter, that decide \emph{which} matches
    survive. Each has a direct physical interpretation in the
    kinematic model of Section~\ref{sec:elevator-kinematics} and
    each guards against a distinct failure mode.
\end{enumerate}

Table~\ref{tab:detect-config} lists every knob, the value we use, and
the failure mode it guards against. The two template-grid blocks come
first because they constrain every later stage; the two acceptance
blocks follow in pipeline order (detection, then pair filter).

\paragraph{How the values were picked.} The template-grid bounds
$(W_{\min},\,W_{\max},\,f_{\min},\,f_{\max})$ were pinned first from
the kinematic analysis in Section~\ref{sec:trapezoid-detect}: for
commercial limits $j_{\max}\!\approx\!1.3$~m/s$^3$,
$a_{\max}\!\approx\!1.0$~m/s$^2$, $v_{\max}\!\approx\!1.5$~m/s, the
realistic ranges of $W$ and $f$ are approximately $[0.8,\,2.5]$~s and
$[0,\,0.7]$ respectively (Equations
\eqref{eq:W-from-kinematics}--\eqref{eq:f-from-kinematics}); the grid
bounds $[0.4,\,3.0]$~s and $[0.05,\,0.80]$ pad these by a safety
margin on each side and were then held fixed.

With the template grid fixed, we ran a grid search over the six
non-kinematic acceptance thresholds (blocks~C and~D above), pinning
\paramcell{min\_ride\_s}~$=$~10~s (shortest plausible floor-to-floor
ride) and \paramcell{max\_ride\_s}~$=$~120~s (upper bound on realistic
rides). The grid had $2 \times 4 \times 1 \times 3 \times 2 \times 3
= 144$ combinations. Each combination was scored on all 22 training
experiments (415 GT rides total) with the composite metric introduced
in Section~\ref{sec:trapezoid-evaluation}. The winning configuration
shown in Table~\ref{tab:detect-config} emerged at the top of that
table and also produced the highest classical F1~@~IoU~$\ge$~0.5 on
the pooled predictions. Lower thresholds than those listed tended to
add false positives faster than recall; higher thresholds tipped the
failure mix into misses. The picked values are at the ``recall
cliff'' on each axis.

\subsubsection{Detection: Per-Sample Best Template}
\label{sec:trapezoid-detect}

\paragraph{Preprocess.} The raw three-axis accelerometer
$(a_x,a_y,a_z)$ is projected onto the per-session gravity direction to
obtain the signed vertical component $a_{\text{vert}}$, then smoothed
with a centered rolling-mean filter of width
\texttt{SMOOTH\_SEC}$\,=\,0.4$~s to obtain $a(t)$. Working in the
smoothed signal is essential because the matched-filter sweep below
would otherwise lock onto high-frequency noise.

\paragraph{Template shape and its link to the kinematics.} We consider
a grid of trapezoidal pulse templates $\tau_{W,f}(t)$ parameterised by a
half-width $W\in\mathcal{W}$ and a flat fraction $f\in\mathcal{F}$,
\begin{equation}
  \tau_{W,f}(t) =
  \begin{cases}
    1 & |t| \le fW, \\
    \dfrac{W - |t|}{(1-f)\,W} & fW < |t| \le W, \\
    0 & |t| > W,
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:trap-template}
\end{equation}
which is a deliberately simplified version of the first acceleration
lobe of \eqref{eq:elevator-template}: the flat plateau stands in for
the $a_{\max}$ cruise, and the two linear ramps for the jerk-limited
onset and offset. The identification with the kinematic parameters
$(j_{\max},\,a_{\max},\,v_{\max})$ is direct. The first lobe of
\eqref{eq:elevator-template} lasts from $t=0$ to $t=2T_j+T_a$, so its
half-width is
\begin{equation}
  W \;=\; T_j + \tfrac{1}{2}T_a
        \;=\; \frac{a_{\max}}{2\,j_{\max}}
              + \frac{1}{2}\,\max\!\Big(0,\;\tfrac{v_{\max}}{a_{\max}} - T_j\Big),
  \label{eq:W-from-kinematics}
\end{equation}
with $T_j=a_{\max}/j_{\max}$ fixed by the jerk limit. The flat fraction
is the ratio of the plateau to the whole lobe,
\begin{equation}
  f \;=\; \frac{T_a}{T_a + 2T_j}
        \;=\; \frac{\max\!\big(0,\;v_{\max}/a_{\max} - T_j\big)}
                   {\max\!\big(0,\;v_{\max}/a_{\max} - T_j\big) + 2T_j}.
  \label{eq:f-from-kinematics}
\end{equation}
Two instructive corner cases of
\eqref{eq:W-from-kinematics}--\eqref{eq:f-from-kinematics}:

\begin{itemize}
  \item A short hop that never reaches $v_{\max}$ has $T_a = 0$, so
    $W = T_j = a_{\max}/j_{\max}$ and $f = 0$ --- a purely triangular
    pulse.
  \item A long ride that spends substantial time at $v_{\max}$ has
    $T_a \gg T_j$, so $W \approx T_a/2$ scales with $H/v_{\max}$ while
    $f \to 1 - 2T_j/T_a$ approaches one.
\end{itemize}
Concretely, for the commercial limits quoted in
\eqref{eq:elevator-bounds} ($j_{\max}\!\approx\!1.3$~m/s$^3$,
$a_{\max}\!\approx\!1.0$~m/s$^2$, $v_{\max}\!\approx\!1.5$~m/s) we have
$T_j\!\approx\!0.77$~s, so $W$ in this dataset lives essentially in
the range $[T_j,\,T_j+T_v/2] \approx [0.8,\,2.5]$~s and $f$ in
roughly $[0,\,0.7]$. The grid is chosen to comfortably bracket that:
$W \in [\texttt{W\_MIN\_S}, \texttt{W\_MAX\_S}] = [0.4,\,3.0]$~s with 30
samples and $f \in [\texttt{F\_MIN}, \texttt{F\_MAX}] = [0.05,\,0.80]$
with 15 samples --- $30 \times 15 = 450$ templates total. Both axes
start at a \emph{positive} floor: $W_{\min}$ is a hair below the
smallest physically plausible half-jerk-ramp $T_j$, and $f_{\min}=0.05$
excludes the degenerate exactly-triangular pulse whose derivative is
discontinuous (the matched filter's score for a pure triangular
template is unstable under any smoothing).

\paragraph{Whole-signal sweep.} For every $(W,f)$ we run a normalised
matched filter over the entire smoothed stream, yielding per sample $i$
the least-squares amplitude and coefficient of determination
\begin{equation}
  \hat A_{W,f}(i) =
    \frac{\langle a_{[i]},\,\tau_{W,f}\rangle}{\|\tau_{W,f}\|^2},
  \qquad
  R^2_{W,f}(i) = 1 - \frac{\|a_{[i]} - \hat A_{W,f}(i)\,\tau_{W,f}\|^2}
                                 {\|a_{[i]}\|^2},
\end{equation}
where $a_{[i]}$ denotes the length-$2W$ signal window centred at
sample~$i$. We then collapse the grid to per-sample summaries:
\begin{align}
  R^2(i),\hat A(i)         &= \text{unsigned argmax across } (W,f), \\
  R^2_{+}(i),\hat A_{+}(i) &= \text{argmax restricted to } \hat A_{W,f}(i) > 0, \\
  R^2_{-}(i),\hat A_{-}(i) &= \text{argmax restricted to } \hat A_{W,f}(i) < 0.
\end{align}
The per-sign arrays are kept so that the diagnostic UI can show
\emph{why} one sign lost even when the unsigned argmax picked the other.

\paragraph{Peak-pick.} Candidates are strict interior local maxima of
$R^2(i)$ gated by the amplitude floor
$|\hat A(i)| \ge \texttt{min\_peak\_abs\_a}$ and the R² threshold
$R^2(i) \ge \texttt{r2\_peak\_thresh}$, followed by a small-radius
non-maximum suppression of \texttt{nms\_radius\_s} (default $0.5$~s).
This first NMS kills duplicate peaks that a single physical pulse
produces at neighbouring samples.

\paragraph{Same-sign NMS.} A second, larger NMS is applied
\emph{within each sign separately}: two candidates carrying the same
sign $s_i = \operatorname{sign}\hat A(i)$ must be at least
\texttt{same\_sign\_min\_gap\_s} apart (default $20$~s); the higher-R²
candidate wins its window. Real elevator physics does not place two
take-offs or two landings that close together, so this pass removes the
secondary lobes the matched filter sometimes picks up around a true
pulse.

The output of stage~1--4 is \texttt{final\_peaks} --- a list of sample
indices $\{i_k\}$, each tagged with a sign
$s_k \in \{+1,-1\}$ from $\operatorname{sign}\hat A(i_k)$.

\subsubsection{What Goes Wrong with Raw Peaks}
\label{sec:trapezoid-problem}

Running just stages 1--4 of Section~\ref{sec:trapezoid-detect} --- per-
sample sweep plus two NMS passes --- produces a set of
\emph{candidate lobes}, not rides. On our dataset this raw candidate
list fails in three characteristic ways that we could see clearly in
the editor's signed-$R^2$ panel (Figure~\ref{fig:trapezoid-signedr2}):

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{False positives on pedestrian motion.} A brisk step
    sequence, a bag drop, or a door slam produces single-lobe
    accelerometer bursts that clear both the $R^2$ and amplitude
    thresholds. In isolation they look identical to an elevator
    take-off. In Figure~\ref{fig:trapezoid-signedr2} these are the
    orange / slate dots outside the committed interval.
  \item \textbf{Mis-paired lobes.} Two unrelated high-$R^2$ peaks with
    opposite signs can sit within the admissibility window, and on
    template-per-peak arguments they would be happily paired. But
    their individual best templates
    $(W_{+}^\star, f_{+}^\star) \ne (W_{-}^\star, f_{-}^\star)$ disagree
    ---the ``take-off'' lobe was actually a footstep with $W\!\approx\!0.5$~s
    and the ``landing'' lobe was an elevator deceleration with
    $W\!\approx\!1.5$~s. A real ride has a single cabin producing both
    lobes with the \emph{same} underlying shape.
  \item \textbf{Swallowed short rides.} A ``big'' candidate pair
    stretching over a long pause between two physically distinct short
    rides can out-score the two correct short-ride pairs if we rank
    pairs only by mean $R^2$. The big pair claims the whole time
    window, making the two real rides look like false positives on its
    shoulders.
\end{enumerate}

Raising the per-peak thresholds does not fix (1): the false-positive
peaks are not low-$R^2$, they are structurally identical to real lobes
until we look at their \emph{partner}. Raising the pair-gap bounds
does not fix (2): the gap itself is fine; it is the shape agreement
that is off. And ranking pairs differently does not fix (3) unless we
make the committed set mutually compatible.

The clearing algorithm in Section~\ref{sec:trapezoid-pair} addresses
all three at once: (1) a lone candidate without an opposite-sign
partner never survives --- the filter is defined on pairs, not lobes;
(2) the \emph{shared-shape} joint fit below collapses the two lobes
onto a single $(W, f)$ template before scoring, so mismatched pairs
score poorly and are rejected; (3) the greedy resolver enforces
interval non-intersection, so two short-ride pairs with the same
total score as one overlapping long pair always win as a pair.

\subsubsection{Pair Filter: Lobe Pairing and Conflict Resolution}
\label{sec:trapezoid-pair}

At this point the detector has produced a list of candidate lobes:
single-template correlation peaks, each tagged with a sign
($+$ for a take-off ramp, $-$ for a landing ramp), a fit amplitude $A$,
and a per-sample correlation score $R^2$. A lobe on its own is not a
ride. A ride is a \emph{pair}: a take-off followed by a landing, with
compatible shape and amplitude. The pair filter is the six-gate
decision procedure that turns the lobe list into final ride intervals.

Think of every opposite-sign lobe pair $(i_1, i_2)$ with
$i_1 < i_2$ as a \emph{hypothesis} that has to clear six gates in
order. Any gate it fails, the hypothesis is discarded. Pairs that
survive are then ranked and committed under a non-overlap rule.

\paragraph{Gate 1 --- Temporal gap.}
The take-off and landing must be separated by a realistic ride
duration:
\[
  \texttt{min\_ride\_s} \;\le\; t(i_2) - t(i_1) \;\le\; \texttt{max\_ride\_s}
  \qquad (\text{default } [0,\;30]\text{ s}).
\]
The lower bound rejects a single lobe from being paired with itself;
the upper bound caps the brute-force ``super pair'' in which a
take-off from one ride gets paired with a landing many rides later.

\paragraph{Gate 2 --- Shared-shape joint R².}
This is the gate that tells a real ride (two lobes cut from the same
cabin motion profile) apart from two unrelated spikes. For each grid
template $\tau_{W,f}$ we place one copy at $i_1$ and one at $i_2$ and
fit a \emph{single} amplitude $A$ to \emph{both} lobes simultaneously.
Writing $a_k$ for the $k$th lobe window, $P_k = \|a_k\|^2$,
$u_k = s_k\,\langle a_k, \tau_{W,f}\rangle$, and $N = \|\tau_{W,f}\|^2$,
the closed-form least-squares solution is
\begin{equation}
  A^\star(W,f) \;=\; \frac{u_1 + u_2}{2N},
  \qquad
  R^2_k(W,f) \;=\; 1 \;-\; \frac{P_k - 2 A^\star u_k + (A^\star)^2 N}{P_k},
\end{equation}
and the pair's score is the mean of the two per-lobe $R^2$,
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{score}(W,f) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(R^2_1(W,f) + R^2_2(W,f)\bigr).
  \label{eq:pair-score}
\end{equation}
We search the grid and keep the best cell $(W^\star, f^\star)$; the
pair passes gate 2 iff
\[
  \mathrm{score}(W^\star, f^\star)
  \;\ge\; \texttt{joint\_r2\_thresh}
  \qquad (\text{default } 0.90).
\]
The shared-shape constraint is the whole point: two unrelated walking
spikes may each individually correlate well with \emph{some} template,
but they rarely agree on the \emph{same} $(W, f)$. A genuine ride's
lobes come from the same cabin profile and agree by construction.

\paragraph{Gate 3 --- Shared amplitude.}
The jointly fit amplitude $A^\star$ must clear a noise floor:
\[
  A^\star \;\ge\; \texttt{min\_pair\_abs\_a}
  \qquad (\text{default } 0.30\ \mathrm{m/s^2}).
\]
Low-amplitude matches are either noise that happened to align with the
template, or rides so short that the velocity never saturated. The
floor is intentionally below the single-lobe amplitude gate
(\texttt{min\_peak\_abs\_a}~$= 0.25$): a shared-amplitude fit averages
out per-lobe noise, so admitting slightly weaker candidates here is
safe.

\paragraph{Gate 4 --- Heatmap energy.}
A genuine ride illuminates a \emph{broad band} of the $(W, f)$ grid
because the lobes tolerate small template mismatch. A noisy accidental
match scores high on only a tiny sliver of the grid and is near-zero
everywhere else. We measure grid breadth with the \emph{heatmap
energy},
\[
  E \;=\;
  \frac{1}{|\text{grid}|} \sum_{(W,f)}
    \max\bigl(\mathrm{score}(W,f),\; 0\bigr),
\]
i.e.\ the mean of clamped pair $R^2$ over every grid cell, and require
\[
  E \;\ge\; \texttt{heatmap\_energy\_thresh}
  \qquad (\text{default } 0.40).
\]
This gate is the biggest source of FP rejections in practice: a
walking-artefact pair can hit $R^2 = 0.95$ at the argmax cell while
averaging $E \approx 0.05$ over the whole grid, whereas a real ride
averages $E \approx 0.5$--$0.7$.

\paragraph{Gate 5 --- Quiet middle.}
Between the take-off and landing lobes the elevator cabin cruises at
constant velocity, so $a_{\text{vert}}$ is close to zero. A walking
pair has continuous motion in the same window. We compute the RMS of
$a_{\text{smooth}}$ over the inter-lobe plateau
$\bigl[\,t(i_1) + W^\star,\; t(i_2) - W^\star\,\bigr]$ and require
\[
  \mathrm{RMS}_{\text{middle}}
  \;\le\; \texttt{quiet\_middle\_ratio} \cdot A^\star
  \qquad (\text{default ratio } 0.5).
\]
The threshold is \emph{relative} to the pair's own amplitude so the
gate scales automatically with ride strength: a $2\,\mathrm{m/s^2}$ ride
is allowed $1\,\mathrm{m/s^2}$ of middle noise; a $0.5\,\mathrm{m/s^2}$
ride only $0.25\,\mathrm{m/s^2}$. Adding this filter roughly halved the
false-positive count in the tuning round (Section~\ref{sec:trapezoid-iterations})
and was what enabled us to loosen the upstream amplitude gates without
admitting walking artefacts.

\paragraph{Gate 6 --- Greedy conflict resolution.}
Pairs that clear gates 1--5 are \emph{candidates}, not yet final. A
lobe can legitimately belong to only one ride, and two accepted rides
should not overlap in time. We rank the candidate list by a score that
slightly favours shorter pairs,
\[
  \mathrm{rank}(i_1, i_2)
  \;=\;
  \mathrm{score}(W^\star, f^\star)
  \;-\; \lambda \cdot \bigl(t(i_2) - t(i_1)\bigr),
  \qquad \lambda = 0.01\,\mathrm{s}^{-1},
\]
and walk it in descending order. A candidate is committed iff
\begin{enumerate}
  \item neither of its two lobes is already committed to an
    earlier-accepted pair, \emph{and}
  \item its time interval $[t(i_1), t(i_2)]$ does not intersect any
    already-committed interval.
\end{enumerate}
The duration term is what defeats the ``super pair'': a pair spanning
the take-off of ride 1 and the landing of ride 6 scores essentially
the same joint $R^2$ as any single-ride pair (same cabin, same shape)
but is many times longer, so $\lambda \cdot \Delta t$ drops it below
the shorter, correct pairs. The second commit condition is the subtle
one: ``no shared lobe'' alone would still admit a long pair alongside
the short rides it swallows, because they share no endpoint; the
interval non-intersection rule gives the higher-ranked partition a
veto over its temporal territory.

\paragraph{Output of the filter.}
Every committed pair becomes one ride prediction. Its type is
\texttt{up} when $s_1 = +1$ (the take-off came first) and
\texttt{down} otherwise. Its endpoints are derived from the winning
template half-width $W^\star$ as described in
Section~\ref{sec:trapezoid-endpoints}. Its confidence is the joint
score $\mathrm{score}(W^\star, f^\star)$. Lobes that never reached a
committed pair are dropped.

\subsubsection{Segment Endpoints}
\label{sec:trapezoid-endpoints}

Each committed pair supplies two things: a pair of lobe-\emph{centre}
samples $(i_1, i_2)$ and the winning shared-shape template
$(W^\star, f^\star)$ that explains both lobes simultaneously. The
predicted ride interval is not the centre-to-centre span --- it is the
union of the two trapezoidal lobes themselves, from the \emph{start of
the first trapezoid} to the \emph{end of the second trapezoid}:
\begin{equation}
  [\,t_{\text{start}},\, t_{\text{end}}\,]
  \;=\;
  [\,t(i_1) - W^\star,\; t(i_2) + W^\star\,].
  \label{eq:trap-endpoints}
\end{equation}
Recall from \eqref{eq:trap-template} that $W^\star$ is the
\emph{half-width} of the winning template: a lobe centred at $t(i_k)$
covers $[\,t(i_k) - W^\star,\; t(i_k) + W^\star\,]$. Equation
\eqref{eq:trap-endpoints} therefore pushes $t_{\text{start}}$ back to
the foot of the take-off ramp and $t_{\text{end}}$ forward to the foot
of the landing ramp --- i.e.\ ``centre~$-$~width'' for the first lobe
and ``centre~$+$~width'' for the second --- so the predicted interval
encloses both jerk-limited ramps \emph{and} their plateaus.

The gap between the two lobe centres, $t(i_2) - t(i_1)$, remains the
physically meaningful quantity: it is the inter-pulse spacing whose
proportionality to $H/v_{\max}$ is given by
\eqref{eq:elevator-durations}. The ride type is read off from the
first-lobe sign: \texttt{ride\_type}~$=$~\texttt{up} if $s_1 = +1$,
else \texttt{down}.

A useful consequence of widening the interval to
\eqref{eq:trap-endpoints} is that it tracks the physical take-off and
landing rather than an internal reference point, so the predicted
interval aligns naturally with the barometer-based GT labels
(Section~\ref{sec:barometer-algorithm}) up to the brief door-open tail
the GT intentionally pads. The evaluator still scores overlap
fractionally --- a GT and prediction ``match'' if either their absolute
overlap is at least a second or their overlap covers at least $30\%$
of the shorter interval --- so a correct-but-tight prediction is not
penalised for missing the padding.

\subsubsection{Segmentation--Prediction Improvement: the $\varepsilon$ pad}
\label{sec:segment-pad-eps}

\paragraph{Why the tight endpoints of
\eqref{eq:trap-endpoints} are not quite right for the downstream
integrators.}
Both accelerometer-only $\Delta h$ predictors --- ZUPT
(\S\ref{sec:algo-zupt}) and trapezoid-pulse-pair
(\S\ref{sec:algo-trapezoid}) --- consume the segmenter's
emitted interval $[t_{\text{start}},\,t_{\text{end}}]$ and
\emph{double-integrate} the vertical acceleration over it to
estimate $\Delta h$. A tight interval whose left edge coincides exactly with
the foot of the take-off ramp inevitably clips the leading few
samples of acceleration (smoothed signals have finite rise time;
gravity-subtraction residuals bleed slightly outside the ramp
support; the jerk-limited profile doesn't truly reach zero at
$\pm W^\star$). The symmetric story holds at the landing ramp.
Clipped tails mean a systematically low $\int a\,\mathrm{d}t$ and
therefore a systematically low $\Delta h$.

\paragraph{Fix.} Widen the emitted interval by a small constant
$\varepsilon$ on each side:
\begin{equation}
  [\,t_{\text{start}},\, t_{\text{end}}\,]
  \;=\;
  [\,t(i_1) - W^\star - \varepsilon,\;
    t(i_2) + W^\star + \varepsilon\,].
  \label{eq:trap-endpoints-eps}
\end{equation}
The pair filter exposes this as a single knob,
\texttt{segment\_pad\_eps\_s}, with default $0.25\,\text{s}$. Setting
$\varepsilon = 0$ reproduces \eqref{eq:trap-endpoints} exactly.

\paragraph{Calibration.} We calibrated $\varepsilon$ by treating
every GT ride as a noise-free segmentation output and measuring how
the downstream predictors' accuracy depended on the pad width. For
each GT ride $(t_{\text{GT-start}}, t_{\text{GT-end}})$ with known
height-difference $\Delta h_{\text{GT}}$ (from the barometer label;
see Section~\ref{sec:barometer-algorithm}) and for each
$\varepsilon$ on the grid $\{0.00, 0.25, 0.50, \ldots, 3.00\}$\,s, we:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item extracted the ACC slice
    $[\,t_{\text{GT-start}} - \varepsilon,\;
      t_{\text{GT-end}}   + \varepsilon\,]$
    as the ``ride'' input;
  \item extracted $3\,\text{s}$ of ACC before and after as the
    stationary \texttt{pre} / \texttt{post} windows used for
    gravity estimation;
  \item ran both \texttt{ZuptAccelEstimator} and
    \texttt{TrapezoidAccelEstimator} on the triple and recorded
    $|\widehat{\Delta h} - \Delta h_{\text{GT}}|$ per algorithm.
\end{enumerate}
The sweep covers every $(+,\,\text{up}/\text{down})$ ride in the
26-experiment dataset for which the barometer supplied a ground-truth
height. Optimum is the $\varepsilon$ that minimises the mean
absolute error, averaged across accepted predictions from both
algorithms.

\paragraph{Result.}
Table~\ref{tab:epsilon-sweep} shows the combined ranking. The optimum
is $\varepsilon = 0.25\,\text{s}$, where the combined accepted MAE
drops from $1.277\,\text{m}$ at $\varepsilon = 0$ (the pre-fix
endpoints) to $1.194\,\text{m}$ --- a $6.5\%$ reduction. The two
algorithms disagree slightly on the best $\varepsilon$

localises the lobes explicitly from the matched filter so it already
``knows'' where the integrable content lives and needs only a token
pad; the raw ZUPT integrator has no such prior and benefits from
wider context. $0.25\,\text{s}$ is simultaneously the trapezoid
optimum and within noise of the ZUPT optimum, so it's the joint
choice.

\paragraph{Impact on segmentation metrics.}
Widening intervals by $0.25\,\text{s}$ on each side does not change
the classification of any GT in the dataset: clean, missed, and
merged counts remain exactly as in the iter\_07 baseline
(Section~\ref{sec:trapezoid-iterations}). It does improve the
$\mathrm{IoU}$-based F1 at threshold $0.5$ from $0.601$ to $0.705$,
because the padded intervals overlap the GT windows more generously.
In other words the $\varepsilon$ pad is a strict win: no detection
decisions flip, predictors integrate cleaner signal, and the edge
accuracy against barometer GT improves.

\subsubsection{Calibrating the Pair Filter by Iteration}
\label{sec:trapezoid-iterations}

Choosing the eight threshold values addresses only half of the
problem. The structure of the pair filter itself --- specifically,
the rule by which the greedy conflict resolver ranks candidate
pairs --- matters at least as much, and cannot be tuned by a scalar
sweep. We therefore ran a second round of calibration in which the
detection-stage thresholds were frozen at the values of
Table~\ref{tab:detect-config} and \emph{only the pair-filter algorithm}
changed. Ten variants were evaluated against the same 22-experiment
set, each with a single targeted change versus the baseline.

\paragraph{What the baseline looked like.}
The baseline filter ranked accepted pairs by their mean joint $R^2$
and applied the greedy conflict resolver described in
Section~\ref{sec:trapezoid-pair}. On its own, that rule produced a
specific pathology: take-offs from one ride and landings from a
\emph{different} ride several rides later tended to score high joint
$R^2$ simply because both lobes were genuine lift lobes (same
building, same cabin, same shape). The resulting ``super pair''
covered every GT ride in between and, by the
interval-non-intersection rule, blocked the true short-ride pairs
from committing. The failure mode dominated the error mix: 208 of
415 GT rides were swallowed by a merged prediction.
Figure~\ref{fig:pair-filter-baseline} shows the effect visually ---
the long purple blocks of predictions cover many green/red GT
intervals each.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{pair_filter_timeline_baseline.png}
\caption{Baseline pair-filter output on the worst-merge experiment.
Top row: GT rides (green $=$ up, red $=$ down). Bottom row: accepted
predictions (blue $=$ up, purple $=$ down). Long purple bars span
five or more consecutive GT rides each --- the super-pair failure
mode. 50\,\% of all GT rides in the dataset land under a merged
prediction like one of these.}
\label{fig:pair-filter-baseline}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Variants tried.} Each of the ten iterations swapped in a
different acceptance or ranking rule and kept everything else
identical:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Baseline} --- mean-$R^2$ greedy ranking.
  \item \textbf{Duration penalty, light.} Rank by
    $\mathrm{score} - \lambda \cdot \Delta t$ with $\lambda = 0.001$,
    so that at similar scores the shorter pair wins.
  \item \textbf{Duration penalty, medium} ($\lambda = 0.003$).
  \item \textbf{Duration penalty, heavy} ($\lambda = 0.01$). A 100~s
    pair now has to beat a 10~s pair by $\ge 0.9$ $R^2$ to outrank
    it.
  \item \textbf{Mutual nearest neighbour.} Accept a pair only if each
    lobe is the other's nearest admissible opposite-sign peak.
  \item \textbf{Quiet middle.} Reject a pair if the signal between
    the two lobes is noisier than $0.5\,A^\star$ at any point.
  \item \textbf{Min-$R^2$ acceptance.} Require $\min(R^2_1, R^2_2)
    \ge$ \texttt{joint\_r2\_thresh} (rather than the mean).
  \item \textbf{Mutual nearest + duration penalty (medium).}
  \item \textbf{Quiet middle + min-$R^2$.}
  \item \textbf{Kitchen sink.} All four above, stacked.
\end{enumerate}

The score progression across the ten iterations is plotted in
Figure~\ref{fig:pair-filter-progress}.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{pair_filter_progress.png}
\caption{Scores across the ten pair-filter variants. Blue: the
four-mode composite of
Section~\ref{sec:trapezoid-evaluation}. Orange: classical F1 at
IoU~$\ge$~0.5. The duration-penalty family (iterations 2--4)
monotonically improves both scores up to $\lambda = 0.01$, at which
point the score more than doubles versus baseline. Stacking rules
(iter 10) over-regularises the filter and costs recall.}
\label{fig:pair-filter-progress}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{What worked: duration penalty.} The biggest gain came from
the simplest change: subtracting $0.01 \cdot \Delta t$ from a pair's
score before ranking. Intuitively, super pairs and genuine long
rides both score the same joint $R^2$, but super pairs are
arbitrarily long while genuine rides cluster around ten to thirty
seconds; a linear cost in $\Delta t$ collapses that asymmetry.
Empirically the composite score went from $0.097$ at baseline to
$0.228$ at $\lambda = 0.01$, the number of merged GTs halved, and
the number of clean one-to-one matches almost tripled.
Figure~\ref{fig:pair-filter-winner} shows the same experiment as
Figure~\ref{fig:pair-filter-baseline} after the fix: the super pairs
are gone and predictions are of the correct length scale.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{pair_filter_timeline_winner.png}
\caption{After adopting the duration-penalty rule ($\lambda = 0.01$)

Predictions are now individual $\sim$10~s bars. A residual failure

\label{fig:pair-filter-winner}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{What didn't work.} The three ``structural'' variants
(mutual nearest, quiet middle, min-$R^2$) each improved a single
failure axis but hurt another more:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Mutual nearest neighbour} dropped false positives
    sharply --- it refuses any pair where either lobe has a nearer
    admissible opposite --- but missed more genuine rides whose
    lobes happen not to be each other's nearest opposite.
  \item \textbf{Quiet middle} worked on paper but rejected many
    true rides whose cruise phase contained real signal (HVAC
    ripples, cable oscillations, cabin vibration). Recall
    collapsed.
  \item \textbf{Min-$R^2$} barely moved the needle. Mismatched pairs
    already had \emph{both} lobes scoring well individually (each is
    a real lift lobe, just from a different ride); the mean-vs-min
    distinction doesn't discriminate in that regime.
\end{itemize}

Stacking several of these constraints (iter 10) multiplied the
recall hits. The lesson: constraints that prune entire families of
candidates are expensive, while a soft ranking bias like the
duration penalty can tilt the greedy in the right direction without
categorically rejecting anyone.

\subsubsection{Detection-Stage Tuning: Triangle Row, $W$ Floor,
Amplitude Floor}
\label{sec:trapezoid-detect-tuning}

A final tuning round on the full $498$-ride GT set revisited three
threshold-grade knobs from Table~\ref{tab:detect-config}, all on
the detection stage. The pair-filter structure
(Section~\ref{sec:trapezoid-iterations}) was held frozen. Three
changes landed; one knob produced a structural fix and the other
two tightened the operating point.

\paragraph{Triangle template row ($f = 0$, iter\_13).}
A one-floor or joined-pulse ride has no cruise phase, so each lobe
collapses to a triangle; the trapezoid kernel of
Equation~\eqref{eq:trap-template} at $f = 0$ matches that shape
exactly. We prepended an explicit $f = 0$ row to the
$(W, f)$ grid built by \paramcell{grid\_f()}. The per-pair
joint-$R^2$ argmax in \paramcell{joint\_pair\_score} then picks
trapezoid vs.\ triangle on a pair-by-pair basis with no separate
branch needed. Across all clean pairs, $10\%$ now prefer
$f = 0$; among missed-but-candidate pairs the figure is $27\%$.
The change is roughly mistake-neutral on the composite
($154 \to 155$) but lifts $\mathrm{IoU}$-F1 at threshold $0.5$
from $0.601$ to $0.703$ because triangle lobes pull endpoints in
where the cruise plateau doesn't exist. This is the largest
single-axis IoU improvement of any tuning round.

\paragraph{Narrow the $W$ floor ($0.4 \to 0.3$~s, iter\_15).}
Diagnostics on the missed rides that did produce candidate pairs
showed \paramcell{pair\_W} pinned at the $0.4$~s lower bound for
many of them --- the grid was being asked for narrower templates
than it was allowed to build. Lowering \paramcell{w\_min\_s} to
$0.3$~s admits those lobes. Combined with the triangle row, this
reduced FPs by $10$ ($59 \to 49$) and dropped the composite
mistake count from $155$ to $147$, a new best.

\paragraph{Lower the peak-amplitude floor ($0.25 \to 0.20$~m/s$^2$,
iter\_16).}
On the remaining $\sim 50$ missed rides that \emph{never}
produced a candidate peak, signed $R^2$ was high (median
$0.94$--$0.95$) but $|\hat A|$ medians sat at
$0.14$--$0.16$~m/s$^2$, just below the gate. Lowering the
\paramcell{min\_peak\_abs\_a} floor to $0.20$~m/s$^2$ admits some
of these. The quiet-middle filter (\S\ref{sec:trapezoid-pair},
Gate 5) and the $0.40$ heatmap-energy gate caught the walking FPs
that might have leaked through, so the change was a small but
pure FP win: $49 \to 46$, mistakes $147 \to 144$. This is the
deployed configuration.

\paragraph{Net effect and ceiling.} Across the iter\_13--iter\_16
round, the composite mistake count fell $154 \to 144$ ($-6.5\%$
versus the iter\_07 baseline) and $\mathrm{IoU}$-F1 at $0.5$
climbed $0.601 \to 0.705$. Subsequent variants explored after
iter\_16 --- aggressively lower amplitude gates, a duration
penalty on long pairs, session-adaptive floors, a nesting-aware
resolver, conditional heatmaps --- either regressed or no-op'd.
The remaining $\sim 50$ rides that produce no candidate peak at
the current floor carry high signed $R^2$ but genuinely low
acceleration on damped/noisy phones (one Pixel~10 unit alone
contributes $\sim 31$ of them). Recovering them appears to
require per-session noise-scaled floors, not a further global
threshold sweep.

\subsubsection{Metric Definitions}
\label{sec:trapezoid-evaluation}

Reporting a single detection F1 on temporal intervals hides several
distinct failure modes we genuinely want to distinguish. We therefore
evaluate every configuration and every iteration with two
complementary metric families.

\paragraph{Four-mode composite.} Given a GT set and a prediction
set, we compute a bipartite overlap graph. Two intervals count as
``matching'' if their absolute overlap is at least one second or
their overlap covers at least $30\%$ of the shorter interval. We
then categorise every GT and every prediction:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Clean} --- one-to-one matches (good).
  \item \emph{Missed} --- a GT with zero overlapping predictions.
  \item \emph{GT-merged} --- a GT that shares its single overlapping
    pred with at least one other GT; symmetrically
    \emph{pred-merged} on the pred side (the failure mode of
    Figure~\ref{fig:pair-filter-baseline}).
  \item \emph{GT-split} --- a GT covered by two or more predictions,
    and the corresponding \emph{pred-split-part} on the pred side.
  \item \emph{False-positive} --- a prediction with zero overlapping
    GTs (lands on ``outside'').
\end{itemize}

The aggregate score is
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{F1}^\ast \;=\;
  \frac{2 \cdot \mathrm{clean}}{2 \cdot \mathrm{clean} + \mathrm{bad}_{\rm GT} + \mathrm{bad}_{\rm pred}},
  \label{eq:f1-like}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{bad}_{\rm GT} = \mathrm{missed} + \mathrm{GT\text{-}merged} + \mathrm{GT\text{-}split}$
and $\mathrm{bad}_{\rm pred} = \mathrm{fp} + \mathrm{pred\text{-}merged} + \mathrm{pred\text{-}split\text{-}part}$.
A perfect detector scores 1. Every failure category counts equally
once, which is what makes the metric discriminating: a merge-heavy
configuration and a miss-heavy configuration can score the same F1
on a pooled IoU evaluation but score very differently on
\eqref{eq:f1-like}, which is what we want when tuning the pair
filter.

\paragraph{Classical IoU-F1 @ 0.5.} For comparability with external
temporal-detection work (PASCAL VOC, COCO temporal tracks,
ActivityNet) we also report the standard F1 at intersection-over-
union $\ge 0.5$: greedily assign each GT its highest-IoU pred, count
a match if IoU $\ge 0.5$, and compute F1 over the pooled TP/FP/FN
counts. This second number is directly comparable to any
off-the-shelf interval-detection baseline; it is the orange curve in
Figure~\ref{fig:pair-filter-progress}.

\paragraph{Why both.} The composite score
\eqref{eq:f1-like} tells us which failure mode dominates and guides
algorithmic changes (it was what surfaced the super-pair problem);
the IoU-F1 tells us whether the \emph{edges} of the intervals are
well-placed once a match exists, and how the detector compares to
generic temporal baselines. On our dataset the two curves track each
other across iterations, which is a sanity check that the composite
isn't gamed by quirks of the four-mode definition.

\subsubsection{Dataset and Protocol}
\label{sec:seg-eval-dataset}

The evaluation dataset is the union of every recording in
\texttt{rawData/} that carries either a barometer-derived or a
hand-labelled GT track: $\SegPooledNExp$ experiments,
$\SegPooledNGt$ \texttt{up}/\texttt{down} rides, five distinct
phone models (Google Pixel~10, Samsung Galaxy~S23, Samsung
Galaxy~A23, Samsung Galaxy~Z~Flip6, Xiaomi 22101320I) across six

Bar-Ilan~2 Herzelia, Haari~3) plus the held-out Beit~Yitzchaki
Raanana site. The split rule (\texttt{src/data/loader/pipeline.py},
\texttt{classify\_experiment\_type}) is purely lexical on the folder
name, so reruns are deterministic.

For each experiment, sensor frames and the GT-ride list are loaded
via \texttt{getExperimentData} (cached CSV path under
\texttt{structuredData/data/}). The active iter\_16 detector is
invoked through the standard \texttt{Segmenter.detect} dispatcher
with no overrides; the chip-spec amplitude floor is enabled and uses
each experiment's recorded phone model. Metrics are aggregated using
\texttt{IntervalPredictionMetrics.from\_intervals} on each experiment
separately and then summed; IoU-F1@0.5 is computed on a pooled stream
of intervals (each experiment offset by $10^9$~s before pooling, so
inter-experiment matches are impossible).

\subsubsection{Why the Three Outliers Fail: Per-Experiment
Diagnosis}
\label{sec:seg-eval-outlier-diagnosis}

to ``phones whose chip-level acceleration noise floor swallows the
lift lobes''. That story is half right. Re-running the deployed
detector on each of the three recordings inside the editor

\paragraph{Cross-cutting takeaways.}

The error tail in \S\ref{sec:seg-eval-cleaned} is not a single
phenomenon. It is two stacked failure modes the deployed
operating point is not robust to:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Damped-amplitude failure}
  (\texttt{milleniumHotel/Pix10},
  \texttt{milleniumHotel/A23}). The trapezoid lobes are
  geometrically present but their amplitude lives inside the
  phone's accelerometer noise band, so the global
  \texttt{min\_peak\_abs\_a} = $0.20$~m/s$^2$ and
  \texttt{min\_pair\_abs\_a} = $0.30$~m/s$^2$ floors swallow
  them. This is what the original write-up describes.
  Diagnostically: median peak $|A|$ on the missed rides is below
  or right at the floor, peak $R^2$ is fine, joint $R^2$ is fine
  whenever a pair manages to form.
  \item \textbf{Shape-mismatch failure}
  (\texttt{BarIlan2/Pix10}). The lobes are far above the
  amplitude floor but a single shared $(W, f)$ trapezoid cannot
  fit both halves of the ride simultaneously --- typically
  because the acceleration lobe is short while the deceleration
  lobe is long, or vice versa. The pair stage's joint-$R^2$ gate
  rejects the pair, and the heatmap-energy gate rejects the
  same pairs as a secondary effect. Diagnostically: median peak
  $|A|$ comfortably above the floor, joint $R^2$ in the
  $0.75$--$0.88$ band, heatmap energy in the $0.30$--$0.45$
  band.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Concrete fixes.}

These two modes call for two separate levers. The fixes below
are ordered by expected per-mode coverage and implementation
cost.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Per-session noise-scaled amplitude floor.}
  Replace the constant \texttt{min\_peak\_abs\_a} (and pair
  counterpart) with $k \cdot \widehat{\sigma}_a$, where
  $\widehat{\sigma}_a$ is estimated from the in-session
  \texttt{outside}-tagged windows of $a_\mathrm{vert}$ and $k$
  is the existing \texttt{noise\_sigma\_multiplier} ($\sim 6$,
  see Table~\ref{tab:detect-config}). The phone-aware
  noise-floor hook in \texttt{detect.\_apply\_phone\_noise\_floor}
  already tightens the floor when a phone model is named; the
  proposal is to additionally \emph{loosen} it (down to a
  hard physical minimum, e.g.\ $0.05$~m/s$^2$) when the
  in-session $\widehat{\sigma}_a$ is well below the global
  default. This recovers the
  \texttt{milleniumHotel} sessions: median peak $|A|$ on the
  Pixel\,10 missed rides is $0.10$~m/s$^2$, so a floor of
  $6 \cdot 0.012 \approx 0.07$~m/s$^2$ would let those lobes
  through while keeping the joint-$R^2$ gate in place to filter
  any walking artefacts that get picked up at the lower floor.
  \item \emph{Per-lobe template shapes.} The pair filter
  currently constrains both lobes to share $(W, f)$. Relaxing
  this to two independent $(W_1, f_1)$, $(W_2, f_2)$ fits while
  still requiring matching amplitudes
  $|A_1| \approx |A_2|$ (which the physics dictates: the lift
  starts and stops with the same passenger mass and the same
  motor torque) would let a short acceleration lobe pair with a
  long deceleration lobe. The joint $R^2$ would be redefined as
  the pooled variance-explained over both individual fits, and
  the heatmap-energy gate would be replaced with a per-lobe
  energy threshold. This recovers the Bar-Ilan~2 ride class
  without weakening the existing shared-shape gate on rides
  where it works. Implementation cost is moderate: one new
  scoring routine in \texttt{pair\_filter.joint\_pair\_score}
  and a re-fit of the iter\_16 thresholds against the cleaned
  per-GT scoreboard data.
  \item \emph{High-rise-aware ride duration prior.} The
  Bar-Ilan~2 lobe-asymmetry is also captured by a single scalar
  --- the ratio of acceleration-lobe half-width to
  deceleration-lobe half-width. A soft prior at the pair stage
  that allows $W_2 / W_1 \in [0.3, 3.0]$ (so a $5\times$
  asymmetry is rejected, but a $2\times$ asymmetry is admitted)
  would catch the same population at lower implementation cost
  than (2). Useful as a fallback if the per-lobe fit proves
  expensive to add.
  \item \emph{Loosen the heatmap-energy floor on
  high-amplitude pairs.} The heatmap-energy gate is most useful
  on weak-signal walking artefacts; on rides where the pair
  $|A|$ is comfortably above $0.5$~m/s$^2$ it is firing as a
  redundant copy of the joint-$R^2$ gate. Conditioning the
  $0.40$ floor on pair amplitude (e.g.\ disabling it for
  $|A| > 1.0$~m/s$^2$) would recover several Bar-Ilan~2 rides
  at no measurable false-positive cost, and is a one-line
  change in \texttt{pair\_filter.predict\_pairs}.
\end{itemize}

The unifying observation across these proposals is that the
deployed operating point was tuned for the modal phone and the
modal building: a clean accelerometer trace and a $\sim 5$--$10$
floor ride. The three outliers occupy two corners of the
deployment space the modal tuning does not protect --- a
high-rise long-cruise asymmetric lobe pair, and a
quiet-chip damped lobe pair. Each corner has a directly
addressable lever, and the levers do not interact: fixing the
amplitude-floor mode does not weaken the shape-mode discriminant,
and vice versa.

\subsubsection{Why These Constraints Hold: Per-GT Score
Distributions}
\label{sec:seg-eval-constraints}

The pair filter rejects a candidate pair if any of three pair-level
gates fails: joint $R^2 \ge 0.90$
(\texttt{joint\_r2\_thresh}), pair amplitude
$|\hat A| \ge 0.30$~m/s$^2$ (\texttt{min\_pair\_abs\_a}), and
mean clamped joint-$R^2$ across the $(W, f)$ grid
$\ge 0.40$ (\texttt{heatmap\_energy\_thresh}). At the detection
stage two more gates filter peak candidates: peak signed
$R^2 \ge 0.40$ (\texttt{r2\_peak\_thresh}) and peak amplitude
$|\hat A| \ge 0.20$~m/s$^2$
(\texttt{min\_peak\_abs\_a}). Each of these thresholds was chosen
in the iter\_07 / iter\_15 / iter\_16 tuning rounds; the histograms
below show the per-GT diagnostic scores recorded by the iter\_16
runner (see
\texttt{src/segmentation/algorithms/improvement\_iterations/iter\_16\_lower\_peak\_a/per\_gt.csv})
coloured by the GT's classification status. Where the clean
density mass sits on one side of the dashed threshold and the
missed density mass sits on the other, the gate is doing useful
discriminative work --- the constraint earns its keep.

\subsubsection{Pair-Filter Hyperparameter Walkthrough}
\label{sec:seg-eval-walkthrough}

The pair filter exposes seven tunable knobs (six pair-level gates
plus one segment-padding constant). The detector hyper-parameter
table at \S\ref{sec:trapezoid-tunables}
(Table~\ref{tab:detect-config}) lists their deployed iter\_16
values; this walkthrough takes them one at a time, in pipeline
order, and explains why each one exists, how the iteration record
proves it earns its keep, and why the chosen threshold value
sits where it does. Each knob occupies its own page so the
gate-by-gate logic can be read end-to-end without backtracking.

\paragraph{1.\ \texttt{min\_ride\_s} = 0.0~s --- minimum pair
duration.}

\textbf{Need.} A trapezoid pulse pair represents the
acceleration--deceleration signature of a single elevator ride;
the time between the positive lobe (acceleration) and the negative
lobe (deceleration) is the ride's cruise duration. A pair where
that interval collapses to nearly zero is geometrically
implausible --- the elevator would have to brake at the same
instant it finishes accelerating. Without this guard the pair
search would happily emit pairs with overlapping or near-touching
lobes when noise momentarily satisfies the joint-$R^2$ test.

\textbf{Examples.} The deployed value is $0.0$~s because
\texttt{same\_sign\_min\_gap\_s} (5~s) at the peak stage already
forces consecutive same-sign peaks apart and \texttt{w\_min\_s}
($0.30$~s) gives every lobe a non-trivial width, so the effective
floor is already $\sim 0.6$~s without ever invoking
\texttt{min\_ride\_s}. The knob is retained as a no-op safety net:
it is the single line of defence if those upstream gates were ever
loosened. The hyperparameter sweep has it pinned at zero across
every iteration tried (no iteration found a non-zero value useful);
that null result is itself the evidence the knob does not need to
fire today, but its presence cheaply guarantees that no
zero-duration ride can ever leak through.

\textbf{Logic.} The interval test is a hard reject (not a soft
penalty) because a sub-resolution pair has no useful prediction
attached to it --- a downstream integrator running on a
$\sim 0$~s window would produce a meaningless distance. Treating
duration as a hard floor short-circuits the rest of the filter and
saves the cost of evaluating the joint-$R^2$ heatmap on garbage
inputs.

\newpage

\paragraph{2.\ \texttt{max\_ride\_s} = 30.0~s --- maximum pair
duration.}

\textbf{Need.} The greedy pair-search step can in principle
combine a positive lobe from one ride with a negative lobe from a
much later ride if the joint-$R^2$ score happens to clear the
gate. The result is a single ``super-pair'' that swallows multiple
real GTs (the \texttt{pred\_merged} / \texttt{gt\_merged} failure
mode of \S\ref{sec:trapezoid-pair}). \texttt{max\_ride\_s} caps
the temporal extent of any candidate pair, breaking these
super-pairs at their root.

\textbf{Examples.} The super-pair problem is the original failure
mode that motivated rewriting the pair filter altogether. Iter\_00
(baseline) had super-pairs spanning $40$--$70$~s on the Bar-Ilan~2
recordings; raising the cap to $30$~s collapsed them. The
hyperparameter sweep
(Figure~\ref{fig:seg-eval-sweeps}, sub-figure
\texttt{max\_ride\_s}) shows $F1^\ast$ falling sharply as the cap
is lifted past $30$~s --- each additional second of
allowed-pair-extent admits one or two new super-pairs that each
swallow $2$--$3$ GTs. The composite cost is at least $4$--$6$
mistakes per second of additional headroom past the elbow.

\textbf{Logic.} A real elevator ride's cruise interval is bounded
by the building height: at the typical $1.5$--$2.5$~m/s observed
across our buildings, a $30$-floor express ride takes
$\sim 12$--$25$~s, so $30$~s is a generous bound that retains a
safety margin for the few longer rides we have logged in tall
buildings (Acro, Bar-Ilan~2). Going much higher is gratuitous;
going much lower would clip the longest rides in the dataset and
produce \texttt{gt\_split} failures.

\newpage

\paragraph{3.\ \texttt{joint\_r2\_thresh} = 0.90 --- minimum pair
joint $R^2$.}

\textbf{Need.} A candidate pair only earns acceptance if both
lobes individually fit the trapezoid template well. Joint $R^2$
is the geometric mean of \texttt{pos\_r2} and \texttt{neg\_r2}
(both lobes must be good --- not just one). Without this gate any
single strong lobe paired with a noise-fit lobe of the opposite
sign would emit a ride; a single walking step is one example of
exactly such a noise-fit lobe.

\textbf{Examples.} Iter\_21 lowered the threshold to $0.87$,
hoping to recover three rides whose joint $R^2$ sat between
$0.87$ and $0.90$; FPs went up by $7$ at the same time
($46 \to 53$) because seven walking-step pairs slipped through
the relaxed gate. Net mistake count went $144 \to 148$ ---
clear regression. The constraint distribution
(Figure~\ref{fig:seg-eval-pair-constraints}, top-left) makes the
choice visible: clean GTs cluster around $0.93$--$0.99$, and the
missed-with-pair tail clusters at $\le 0.87$. The $0.90$ floor
sits in the empty corridor between the two populations, which is
exactly what a well-chosen threshold should look like. The reject
reason bar (Figure~\ref{fig:seg-eval-reject-reasons}) further
shows that the joint-$R^2$ gate catches more rejected pairs than
any other single gate --- the most discriminative knob in the
filter.

\textbf{Logic.} Joint $R^2$ is the right test (vs. say minimum
or arithmetic mean of the two lobe $R^2$ values) because it
penalises asymmetry: a pair with one $R^2 = 0.99$ lobe and one
$R^2 = 0.70$ lobe gets a joint score of $\sqrt{0.99 \cdot 0.70}
\approx 0.83$ --- which is below the floor, even though the
arithmetic mean ($0.84$) and minimum ($0.70$) might still pass
softer alternatives. The geometric mean is the principled choice
when the two factors are required to be co-good, which is exactly
the regime here: an elevator ride has two well-shaped lobes by
construction.

\newpage

\paragraph{4.\ \texttt{min\_pair\_abs\_a} = 0.30~m/s$^2$ ---
minimum pair amplitude.}

\textbf{Need.} The pair amplitude $|\hat A|$ is the geometric
mean of the two lobe peak amplitudes. Pairs with very small
amplitudes are the regime where the chip-level acceleration noise
of the phone starts to dominate the lobe shape --- you can fit a
trapezoid to almost anything if you let the amplitude shrink. This
gate filters out pairs whose absolute energy is too low to be a
real ride, irrespective of how good the shape match looks.

\textbf{Examples.} The iteration record has six attempts to move
this knob:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{iter\_02} pushed it $0.30 \to 0.40$~m/s$^2$ ---
    only killed $1$ FP, lost $7$ clean GTs; reverted.
  \item \texttt{iter\_04} reverted to $0.30$ and instead added the
    \texttt{quiet\_middle\_ratio} filter, which produced the
    larger $99 \to 50$ FP win.
  \item \texttt{iter\_14} pushed $0.30 \to 0.22$ alongside
    widening the $W$ floor; \texttt{gt\_split} jumped $1 \to 10$
    on Beit Mansour because admitting weak pairs caused
    over-segmentation of long rides.
  \item \texttt{iter\_15} restored $0.30$, kept the $W$ widening;
    new best at the time.
  \item \texttt{iter\_19} retried $0.26$ with a duration penalty
    \--- \texttt{gt\_split} $1 \to 8$ again; reverted.
  \item \texttt{iter\_17} attempted $0.20$ in combination with
    other relaxations, producing the catastrophic
    \texttt{gt\_split} $1 \to 86$ regression.
\end{itemize}
The constraint distribution
(Figure~\ref{fig:seg-eval-pair-constraints}, top-right)
shows the floor sitting at the right edge of the missed-with-pair
mode and just below the clean cluster. The
\texttt{min\_pair\_abs\_a} sweep
(Figure~\ref{fig:seg-eval-sweeps}, sub-figure
\texttt{min\_pair\_abs\_a}) shows the characteristic U-shape
around $0.30$: lower regresses through over-segmentation, higher
regresses through lost recall.

\textbf{Logic.} Walking artefacts and small accelerometer
disturbances live below $0.20$~m/s$^2$; real elevator-ride lobes
on every phone in our dataset (except the three damped sessions
of \S\ref{sec:seg-eval-cleaned}) sit at $0.4$--$1.0$~m/s$^2$.
$0.30$~m/s$^2$ is the inter-cluster gap. Crucially, this is a
\emph{pair}-level floor, not a per-lobe floor: the per-lobe floor
(\texttt{min\_peak\_abs\_a}) is more permissive at
$0.20$~m/s$^2$, which lets one weak lobe survive as long as its
partner is strong enough that the geometric-mean pair amplitude
clears $0.30$.

\newpage

\paragraph{5.\ \texttt{heatmap\_energy\_thresh} = 0.40 ---
mean clamped joint $R^2$ across the $(W, f)$ grid.}

\textbf{Need.} A real elevator ride matches the trapezoid
template across a band of nearby $(W, f)$ combinations --- the
joint-$R^2$ heatmap has a wide peak. A walking-step or other
short-time impulsive artefact may match one specific
$(W^\star, f^\star)$ combination by coincidence but yields a
fragmentary heatmap (high at one cell, near zero everywhere
else). The mean clamped joint-$R^2$ over the heatmap is a single
scalar that captures this multi-template consistency, and the gate
rejects pairs whose heatmap fails to spread.

\textbf{Examples.} \texttt{iter\_03} introduced the heatmap-energy
gate at threshold $0.20$ initially, then settled on $0.40$ ---
FPs dropped $145 \to 99$, the second-largest single gate win in
the project after \texttt{quiet\_middle\_ratio}. The
constraint distribution
(Figure~\ref{fig:seg-eval-pair-constraints}, bottom-left) shows
clean GTs filling a broad density between $0.4$ and $0.9$, with
the rejected tail of fragmentary-heatmap pairs sitting below
$0.4$. The narrow tail explains why this gate has only the
moderate footprint visible in the reject-reason bar
(Figure~\ref{fig:seg-eval-reject-reasons}): joint-$R^2$ catches
most of the obvious bad pairs first, and heatmap energy catches
the residual tail of pairs that cleared joint-$R^2$ but only on
a single template cell.

\textbf{Logic.} The mean is taken over a $30 \times 15$ grid
($n_W \times n_f$) of clamped joint-$R^2$ values; clamping to
$[0, 1]$ before averaging means a few rogue $R^2 < 0$ values
cannot drag the mean below threshold artificially. The threshold
$0.40$ is far enough above the $\sim 0.05$--$0.20$ noise
background, but well below the $0.6$--$0.9$ that real rides
produce, so it sits in the empty band between the two
populations.

\newpage

\paragraph{6.\ \texttt{quiet\_middle\_ratio} = 0.5 --- inter-lobe
plateau RMS limit.}

\textbf{Need.} A real elevator ride has a quiet cruise interval
between its acceleration and deceleration lobes --- once cruise
speed is reached, the accelerometer reading is dominated by
gravity alone and the residual after gravity removal is small.
A walking-step pair, by contrast, has continuous body motion
between the two lobes and the inter-lobe interval is loud. The
quiet-middle gate rejects pairs whose plateau RMS is comparable
to the lobe peak amplitude --- there is no real ``cruise''
between them.

\textbf{Examples.} This is the single biggest change in the
project's iteration record. \texttt{iter\_04} introduced the
filter at \texttt{ratio = 0.5}; FPs dropped $99 \to 50$
(a $-49$ swing in a single iteration, the largest in the
twenty-four-iteration history). \texttt{iter\_20} tried tightening
to $0.45$; the change lost clean rides on a few experiments
where the cruise interval had genuinely loud micro-vibration ---
the floor at $0.5$ has the right balance between rejecting
walking artefacts and tolerating realistic cruise noise. Looking
at the rejected mistake PNGs in the iter\_04 directory makes the
visual story concrete: the rejected pairs are walking steps where
the inter-step interval shows continuous oscillation at amplitudes
similar to the step itself, exactly the regime the gate targets.

\textbf{Logic.} The metric is
$\mathrm{RMS}(\text{plateau}) / |\hat A_{\text{pair}}|$ rather
than an absolute RMS so it scales with the ride amplitude --- a
fast-moving express elevator can tolerate slightly louder cruise
RMS than a slow residential one because the lobe amplitude is
itself larger. Choosing $0.5$ as the dimensionless ratio means
the cruise can have at most half the lobe amplitude in RMS terms,
which corresponds (for a sinusoidal residual) to about $35\%$
peak-to-peak. Above that you are not looking at cruise; you are
looking at body motion.

\newpage

\paragraph{7.\ \texttt{segment\_pad\_eps\_s} = 0.25~s --- emitted
ride interval pad.}

\textbf{Need.} The pair filter outputs ride intervals as
$[t_{c,1} - W - \varepsilon, \; t_{c,2} + W + \varepsilon]$, where
$t_{c,*}$ are the lobe centres and $W$ is the half-width. The
$\varepsilon$ pad gives downstream integrators (ZUPT and the
trapezoid prediction in \S\ref{sec:height-prediction}) a small
margin of pre-ride and post-ride stationary data on either side
of the detected envelope. Without it, the integrator's gravity
calibration would have to use the very edge of the ride itself,
where the lobe is not yet finished decaying --- and gravity
estimation in turn drives the eventual height-difference
estimate.

\textbf{Examples.} \texttt{iter\_12} swept this knob over
$\varepsilon \in \{0.00, 0.10, 0.25, 0.50, 1.00\}$ measuring the
\emph{predictor} MAE on the train set rather than the segmentation
$F1^\ast$, because the knob is a downstream-aware tuning. The
sweep produced a clean optimum at $0.25$~s: at zero the predictor
loses its pre/post stationary windows; at $0.50$~s and beyond the
extra padding starts to include neighbouring outside-ride motion,
introducing bias in the gravity calibration. The
prediction section's per-experiment table
(Table~\ref{tab:per-exp-train}) reports the MAE numbers achieved
with the deployed $\varepsilon = 0.25$.

\textbf{Logic.} A symmetric pad is the right shape because the
gravity-calibration step uses both pre-ride and post-ride
windows; an asymmetric pad would bias the calibration toward
whichever side has more data. The size of the pad is set by the
phone sample rate ($\sim 100$~Hz) and the gravity-calibration
filter time constant ($\sim 0.3$~Hz cutoff requires
$\sim 0.25$--$0.30$~s of data to settle). $0.25$~s is the
minimum pad that buys reliable settling; longer pads buy
diminishing returns and risk reaching into adjacent activity.

\section{Pipeline}
\label{sec:pipeline}

The two preceding chapters describe the segmenter
(\S\ref{sec:segmentation-detection}) and the predictor
(\S\ref{sec:height-prediction}) as standalone components. In
deployment they form a single sensor-to-height pipeline; this
chapter walks the end-to-end flow, then evaluates the joint output
under three different definitions of the input set --- each
answering a different operational question about how the system
behaves once the segmenter's output is fed into the predictor.

\subsection{Flow: Sensors $\to$ Segmenter $\to$ Predictor $\to$
$\Delta h$}
\label{sec:pipeline-diagram}

The pipeline takes a single phone recording and emits a list of
predicted rides, each with a signed $\Delta h$ in meters and a
$90\,\%$ confidence interval. Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline-flow}
captures the chain. The loader hydrates per-sensor frames from raw
sensor logs and (when a barometer was present) derives the
ground-truth ride list. The active accelerometer segmenter
(\texttt{ACC\_TEMPLATE\_MATCH}, iter\_16 config) reads the
accelerometer frame and emits ride intervals. For each ride
interval the active predictor (\texttt{TRAPEZOID\_ACCEL}) consumes
a slice of the accelerometer frame plus a $\pm 3$~s pre/post
stationary window and emits a \texttt{PredictionOutput} carrying
the signed $\Delta h$, the conformal CI half-width, and a quality
verdict.

\subsection{Three Evaluation Views}
\label{sec:pipeline-views}

We evaluate the pipeline under three different definitions of
\emph{which intervals get sent to the predictor} and \emph{which
truth source we score against}. In every view the prediction
itself comes from the same deployed predictor; only the input set
changes.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{GT-segments view.} Bypass the segmenter entirely:
    the predictor reads each \emph{ground-truth} interval
    directly. Truth is the GT row's \texttt{height\_diff\_m}
    (barometer-derived). One score per GT ride; FPs and missed
    rides do not appear by construction. Tells us \emph{how good
    is the predictor itself when given perfect segmentation}.

  \item \textbf{Matched-segmenter view (``segmenter when
    correct'').} Run the segmenter; for every prediction that
    cleanly matches a GT (\S\ref{sec:trapezoid-evaluation}), feed
    the \emph{segmenter's} interval to the predictor. Truth is
    the matched GT row's \texttt{height\_diff\_m}. One score per
    matched (clean) prediction; missed and false-positive
    predictions do not appear. Tells us \emph{how good is the
    prediction when the segmenter has actually identified a ride,
    using the segmenter's slightly imperfect edges}.

  \item \textbf{All-segments-vs-barometer view.} Run the
    segmenter; feed \emph{every} prediction to the predictor,
    including false positives. Truth is the barometer-derived
    $\Delta h$ integrated over the same predicted interval (the
    barometer can answer ``what altitude change actually happened
    here'' on any window, with no need for a label). One score
    per prediction the segmenter emitted. Tells us \emph{how well
    the accelerometer-only predictor agrees with the barometer
    on every window the segmenter actually emits in production},
    independent of whether that window corresponds to a real
    ride.
\end{itemize}

The three views together separate three failure modes the
pipeline can suffer: (1)~the predictor's intrinsic error on a
clean ride (visible in the GT view), (2)~the additional error the
predictor incurs because the segmenter's interval edges are not
exactly the GT edges (the gap between the matched and GT views
isolates this), and (3)~the cost of the segmenter inventing rides
that aren't there (the gap between the matched and all-vs-barometer
views isolates this).

\subsection{Adding the Quality Filter: Pipeline = Segmentation
$+$ Prediction $+$ Quality}
\label{sec:pipeline-quality}

Every \texttt{PredictionOutput} the predictor emits carries an
\texttt{accepted} boolean (\S\ref{sec:height-prediction}, the
quality filter). A downstream consumer that only trusts accepted
predictions sees a different output stream than the raw one ---
fewer rides, but each one vetted. This subsection re-derives the
three views with the quality filter applied, so we can compare
the deployed system as-a-whole (segmentation $+$ prediction $+$
quality) against the unfiltered baseline of
\S\ref{sec:pipeline-headline}.

\paragraph{Accept-rate breakdown.} On the
$\PipeSegTotalN$ segmenter predictions across the full dataset
the quality filter accepts $\PipeSegAcceptedN$ ($\PipeAcceptRateAll$
overall). Stratified by segmentation status:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Clean (true-positive) predictions:}
    $\PipeCleanAcceptedN$ of $\PipePooledMatchedN$ pass
    ($\PipeAcceptRateClean$). The handful of clean rejections
    are mostly short rides on damped phones where the trapezoid
    fit is genuinely poor.
  \item \textbf{False-positive predictions:}
    $\PipeFpAcceptedN$ of $\PipeFpN$ pass
    ($\PipeAcceptRateFp$). \emph{Every single false positive is
    rejected by the quality filter.} The phantom-altitude
    problem of \S\ref{sec:pipeline-fps} essentially disappears
    when the consumer respects the \texttt{accepted} flag.
  \item \textbf{GT-direct predictions} (oracle view, never
    deployed but useful for the GT-segments comparison):
    $\PipeGtAcceptedN$ of $\PipeGtTotalN$ pass
    ($\PipeAcceptRateGt$). The lower acceptance rate here is
    because the oracle includes hard rides on damped phones
    that the segmenter would not have emitted in the first
    place.
\end{itemize}

For the rest of this subsection ``FP'' means \emph{a prediction
that the segmenter labelled as a ride with no GT match
\textbf{and} that the quality filter accepted}, i.e., a phantom
prediction the deployed system actually exposes to its consumer.
Under that definition the dataset has $\PipeFpAcceptedN$ FPs.

\subsection{What These Numbers Say About Trust}
\label{sec:pipeline-verdict}

The picture changes once the quality filter is in the loop. The
deployed system, taken as a whole (segmentation
$+$ prediction $+$ quality), gives the user a $\Delta h$ stream
whose pooled mean absolute error against the barometer is
$\PipePooledAllMaeAcc$~m, with $\PipePooledAllFloorAcc$ of
accepted predictions inside the $\pm 1.5$~m one-floor target ---
sharply better than the $\PipePooledAllMae$~m
/ $\PipePooledAllFloor$ of the unfiltered output. The single
biggest contributor to that improvement is the elimination of
\emph{every} false positive: $\PipeFpAcceptedN$ of $\PipeFpN$
FPs survive the filter, so the phantom-altitude problem
of \S\ref{sec:pipeline-fps} effectively disappears for any
consumer that trusts the \texttt{accepted} flag.

The trade-off is recall on the prediction side: the filter
rejects $\PipeCleanRejectedN$ of $\PipePooledMatchedN$ clean
predictions, leaving $\PipeAcceptRateClean$ of clean rides
visible to the consumer. Combined with the segmenter's own
$\sim 75$--$85\%$ recall (\S\ref{sec:seg-eval-headline}), the
full pipeline recall measured against GT rides is therefore
roughly $0.8 \times 0.92 \approx 0.74$ --- the user sees about
three out of every four real elevator rides, but each one with
high-confidence height numbers and essentially no phantom rides
in between. That is the right operating point for an altitude
estimator that doubles as input to a downstream floor-detection
model: false positives are far worse than missed rides, and the
quality filter trades the former for a small additional helping
of the latter.

The matched-segmenter view shows the predictor performs well
whenever the segmenter has identified a real ride: $\PipePooledMatchedFloor$
of matched predictions land within one floor with median absolute
error $\PipePooledMatchedMedian$~m. The GT-segments view shows
that giving the predictor perfect intervals does \emph{not}
materially improve the floor-hit rate
($\PipePooledGtFloor$ at $\pm 1.5$~m) --- the segmenter's edge
inaccuracy is not the bottleneck. The all-vs-barometer view,
which includes false positives, is what an end-user would
experience as the running altitude estimate, and its
$\PipePooledAllFloor$ within one floor is the unfiltered bar
that the quality flag closes most of the gap on.

\subsection{Three Error Views: Clean, Oracle, and System-Total}
\label{sec:pipeline-views}

Per-ride errors split into three views, each answering a different
operational question:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Clean autonomous} ($n = \PipePooledCleanautoN$).
    Restrict to GT rides the segmenter cleanly matched
    (\S\ref{sec:trapezoid-evaluation}); the predictor ran on the
    segmenter's interval. Tells us \emph{when the segmenter
    works, how good is the height number?}
  \item \textbf{Oracle / user-corrected segmentation}
    ($n = \PipePooledOracleN$). Predictor ran directly on every
    GT interval. Tells us \emph{what does the system look like
    once a human fixes the segmentation?}
  \item \textbf{System-total per GT}
    ($n = \PipePooledSystemtotalN$). For each GT ride, error
    $= |\Delta h_{\rm gt} - \Delta h_{\rm pred}|$ when the
    segmenter caught it, and $|\Delta h_{\rm gt}|$ when it didn't
    (a missed ride contributes its full magnitude to the system's
    error budget --- the autonomous system simply does not estimate
    that ride). Tells us \emph{what does the user actually
    experience, including the cost of missed rides?}
\end{itemize}

False positives sit in a fourth bucket and are accounted
separately: they have no GT $\Delta h$ to compare against, so we
report the magnitude of the spurious $\Delta h$ predictions
themselves --- the ``phantom altitude'' a naive consumer of the
output stream would integrate.

$\PipeTestCleanautoWithinFloor$ within one floor (\emph{better}
than train, consistent with the segmentation generalisation
result of \S\ref{sec:seg-eval-headline}), while system-total
sits at $\PipeTestSystemtotalMae$~m. The conclusion is the same
on both splits: the predictor is trustworthy whenever it
\emph{runs}; the cost is concentrated on the rides the segmenter
fails to detect.

\subsection{The Cost of Misses and the Phantom-Altitude Problem}
\label{sec:pipeline-misses-fps}

The system-total MAE is dominated by two error sources the clean
or oracle views never see: the $|\Delta h|$ of every missed ride
(the autonomous system implicitly estimates zero, which is wrong
by exactly the ride height) and the phantom $\Delta h$ produced
on every false-positive segment (the autonomous system invents a
ride that wasn't there).

\subsection{Trust Verdict: When to Run Autonomously and When a
Human Should Step In}
\label{sec:pipeline-verdict}

The numbers point to a clear two-mode operating recipe:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{On phones with a clean accelerometer trace}
    (every device in the dataset \emph{except} the three damped
    sessions of \S\ref{sec:seg-eval-cleaned}), the autonomous
    pipeline is trustworthy: $\PipePooledCleanautoWithinFloor$ of
    its predictions land inside the one-floor target with median
    absolute error $\PipePooledCleanautoMedian$~m. A user reading
    the output stream gets the right floor count almost every
    time. No human-in-the-loop is required.

  \item \textbf{On damped/noisy-phone recordings}, the
    bottleneck is segmentation, not prediction --- the median
    missed-ride magnitude of $\PipeMissedMedianDh$~m alone
    drives the system-total MAE from
    $\PipePooledOracleMae$~m (oracle) to
    $\PipePooledSystemtotalMae$~m (autonomous). For these
    recordings the right pattern is human-in-the-loop: the
    segmenter offers a candidate ride list, the user adds the
    rides it missed (visible in the GT editor of
    \S\ref{sec:gt-editor}), and the predictor re-runs on the
    edited list --- collapsing the system-total error toward
    the oracle baseline.
\end{enumerate}

The diagnostic that tells the two regimes apart is observable at
runtime without GT: the per-experiment density of
\emph{candidate-pair rejections} from the pair filter
(\S\ref{sec:trapezoid-pair}) jumps by an order of magnitude on
the damped sessions. A future product UI could surface that
density to the user as a trust score, recommending the
human-correction loop only when it crosses a threshold. The
session-adaptive amplitude floor proposed in
\S\ref{sec:trapezoid-detect-tuning} is the algorithmic alternative
to this UX: if it lands, the autonomous regime extends to the
damped phones and the human-in-the-loop second mode collapses to
a niche.
